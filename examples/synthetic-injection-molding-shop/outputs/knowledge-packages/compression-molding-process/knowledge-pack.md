## Process Overview
Compression molding places a measured charge of rubber, plastic, composite, or elastomer compound into a heated mold cavity, closes the mold under force, cures/forms the part, then removes the molded part for post cure, deflash, and inspection as required. In this shop, `CM-50 Wabash Genesis Compression Press` supports `EL-SEAL-12` `035 Compression Mold`.

## Core Principles
Compression molding depends on charge placement, mold temperature, pressure/force, cure time, material rheology, venting/flash control, and part geometry. For elastomer seals, cure state and flash behavior often matter as much as shape formation.

## Equipment / Tooling / System Principles
The press applies controlled force through platens, often heated. The mold defines cavity, parting line, overflow/flash, venting, and geometry. Downstream post cure and deflash can be normal parts of the final part condition.

## Machine-Specific Notes
Public Wabash Genesis material supports the Genesis family as hydraulic presses for compression molding of rubber, plastics, composites, laminating, and bonding. It does not prove exact `CM-50` platen size, heat capability, controls, or shop settings.

## Key Process Variables
- Charge amount and placement: affect fill, flash, trapped air, and knit/flow.
- Mold temperature: affects cure speed, flow, and material properties.
- Force/pressure and closing speed: affect flow, flash, and mold closure.
- Cure time: affects undercure/overcure risk.
- Mold venting and parting-line condition: affect flash and trapped air.
- Material compound condition: affects flow and cure response.

## Process Window Concepts
The process window balances enough heat/time/pressure for complete cure and geometry without excessive flash, degradation, or dimensional shift. Public sources cannot set numeric cure values.

## Materials, Parts, Geometry, And Environment Interactions
Elastomer seals can be sensitive to cure chemistry, compound age, preform size, flash land design, and post-cure requirements. Geometry around thin lips, grooves, and sealing surfaces can affect deflash/inspection.

## Common Failure Modes
Possible issues include undercure, overcure, flash, voids, trapped air, short fill, backrind-like defects, dimensional variation, surface defects, and post-cure/deflash damage.

## Failure Mechanisms
Heat and pressure drive flow and cure. Too little cure may leave weak/unstable material; too much heat/time may damage properties. Excess material or poor parting-line control can create flash. Trapped gas or flow interruption can create voids or defects.

## Practical Heuristics
For seal questions, consider molding, post cure, deflash, and inspection as a chain. Do not assign issues to the press without cure, material, mold, post-cure, deflash, and inspection context.

## Troubleshooting Logic
Separate symptoms by where they can be introduced: molding geometry/cure, post-cure thermal exposure, deflash method, and final inspection. Use actual records before making process conclusions.

## Important Terminology
- Charge/preform: material loaded into the mold.
- Cure: chemical/thermal setting of elastomer or thermoset-like material.
- Flash: excess material at mold parting/overflow.
- Platen: press surface that applies heat/force.
